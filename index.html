🔨 What Is Work?

🚨 The #1 Misconception About "Work"

In everyday life, "working hard" means effort. In physics, work has a precise definition — and you can push a wall all day without doing any work at all!

❌ NOT work (physics)
Holding a heavy bag stationary. Pushing a wall. Carrying a bag horizontally. Force with NO displacement in force's direction.
✅ IS work (physics)
Lifting a bag off the ground. Pushing a box across a floor. Any force that has a component in the direction of motion.

Definition: Work Done by a Constant Force

Work is done when a force moves its point of application through a displacement in the direction of the force.

W = F · d · cos θ where: F = force (N) d = displacement (m) θ = angle between force and displacement W = work done (J = joules)
θ = 0°
Force parallel to motion
W = Fd
(maximum work)
0° < θ < 90°
Force at angle to motion
W = Fd cosθ
(partial work)
θ = 90°
Force ⊥ to motion
W = 0
(no work done!)

➖ Negative Work & Work Against Forces

When Is Work Negative?

Work is negative when the force and displacement point in opposite directions (90° < θ ≤ 180°). The force is removing energy from the object.

θ = 180°: W = Fd cos180° = −Fd (force opposes motion) Common examples of negative work: • Friction on a sliding object (friction opposes motion) • Braking force on a car • Gravity on an object being lifted upward
Friction always does negative work on a moving object — it takes kinetic energy away and converts it to heat.
📈 Work Done by a Variable Force

When Force Is Not Constant — Use the Area Under F-x Graph

When force varies with displacement, W = F·d no longer applies directly. Instead:

Work done = Area under the Force–displacement (F–x) graph

Area under F-x graph = total work done. Different shapes need different area formulas (rectangle, triangle, trapezium).

For a constant force: rectangle area = F × d. For a linearly varying force: triangle or trapezium. For a curved force: you need calculus (integration) or count squares.

⚡ Types of Mechanical Energy

The Two Forms You Need to Know

For CAPE Applied Math, you work with two forms of mechanical energy. Energy is the capacity to do work and is measured in joules (J).

🏃 Kinetic Energy (KE)
KE = ½mv²
Energy of motion. A stationary object has zero KE. Depends on mass AND speed squared.
🏔️ Gravitational PE (GPE)
GPE = mgh
Energy of position. Depends on height above a reference level (usually ground).

Kinetic Energy — KE = ½mv²

KE = ½ m v² m = mass in kg v = speed in m/s KE is in joules (J) Doubling mass → KE doubles Doubling speed → KE quadruples (×4)! ← v is squared!

v is squared in the formula! A car at 60 mph has 4× the KE of a car at 30 mph — that's why speeding is so dangerous.

Gravitational Potential Energy — GPE = mgh

GPE = m g h m = mass in kg g = 9.8 m/s² (or 10 m/s² if specified) h = vertical height above reference level (m) GPE is in joules (J)

GPE depends on vertical height h — not the path taken to get there.

Always measure h vertically. On a slope of length L at angle α: h = L sinα (the vertical component, not the slope length itself).
🔁 Conservation of Energy

The Principle of Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be converted from one form to another. The total energy of a closed system is constant.

For a mechanical system (no friction / air resistance): KE + GPE = constant (Total Mechanical Energy) Loss in GPE = Gain in KE (as object falls) Gain in GPE = Loss in KE (as object rises)
When friction IS present, mechanical energy is NOT conserved — some energy converts to heat. The total energy of the universe is still conserved, but you lose useful mechanical energy.

🔄 The Work-Energy Principle

The Most Important Theorem in This Module

The net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Net Work Done = ΔKE = KE_final − KE_initial W_net = ½mv² − ½mu² where: u = initial speed, v = final speed
This means:
W_net > 0 → object speeds up (KE increases)
W_net < 0 → object slows down (KE decreases)
W_net = 0 → object moves at constant speed
Object stationary → W needed = ½mv² − 0 = ½mv²

Proof from Newton's 2nd Law

This isn't just a formula — it comes directly from F = ma and kinematics:

Step 1: Newton's 2nd Law: F = ma
Step 2: Kinematics: v² = u² + 2as → a = (v²−u²)/(2s)
Step 3: Substitute: F = m(v²−u²)/(2s)
Step 4: Rearrange: Fs = ½mv² − ½mu²
∴ W = ΔKE ✓

Connecting Work, KE and GPE

When friction is present, energy is lost to heat. Tracking all the energy:

Work done by all forces = ΔKE For a system with gravity and friction: W_applied + W_gravity + W_friction = ΔKE W_gravity = −ΔGP E = mg(h₁ − h₂) W_friction = −f × d (always negative) ∴ W_applied − mgh − fd = ½mv² − ½mu²
When friction is present: Energy IN = Energy OUT + Energy lost to friction. This is the most general form of the work-energy theorem.

💡 What Is Power?

Power = Rate of Doing Work

Power measures how quickly work is done — or how quickly energy is transferred.

P = Work done / time = W / t P = F × v (when force and velocity are parallel) Units: watts (W) = joules per second (J/s) kilowatts (kW) = 1000 W megawatts (MW) = 1 000 000 W
P = W/t
Use when you know total work done and time taken.
e.g. "a motor does 6000 J in 20 s → P = 300 W"
P = Fv
Use when you know the driving force and current speed.
e.g. "car engine force 4000 N at 30 m/s → P = 120 kW"

Why P = Fv? (Where It Comes From)

Start with: P = W/t
Work done: W = F × d
Substitute: P = Fd/t
But d/t = v: P = Fv ✓
P = Fv gives the instantaneous power at a moment when the speed is v. If speed changes, P changes too.

🏎️ Maximum (Terminal) Speed — A Key Exam Topic

A vehicle reaches maximum speed when the driving force exactly equals the resistance force — so the net force is zero, meaning zero acceleration.

At maximum speed v_max: Driving force = Resistance force F = R → a = 0 And: P = F × v_max = R × v_max ∴ v_max = P / R
A vehicle with engine power P and total resistance R has maximum speed v_max = P/R. If resistance doubles, maximum speed halves (for same power).

At v_max, the P/v curve meets the resistance line. The vehicle can no longer accelerate.

Question 6 MEDIUM
A cricket ball at the Warner Park ground of mass 0.16 kg is thrown vertically upward with speed 25 m/s. Using energy conservation, find the maximum height reached. (g = 10 m/s²)

At maximum height, all KE has converted to GPE. Set ½mv² = mgh and solve for h.

h = 31.25 m

At launch: KE = ½ × 0.16 × 25² = ½ × 0.16 × 625 = 50 J

At max height: all KE → GPE, KE = 0

GPE = mgh: 50 = 0.16 × 10 × h → h = 50/1.6 = 31.25 m

Note: mass cancelled out! Any ball thrown at 25 m/s reaches the same height.

Question 7 MEDIUM
A tourist zip-lines from Kittitian Hill starting from rest at height 45 m. Assuming a smooth (frictionless) ride, find the speed at the bottom. (g = 10 m/s²)

Use conservation of energy: GPE lost = KE gained → mgh = ½mv² → v = √(2gh).

v = 30 m/s

Conservation: mgh = ½mv²

Simplify: gh = ½v² → v² = 2gh = 2 × 10 × 45 = 900

Answer: v = √900 = 30 m/s

Question 8 MEDIUM
A market vendor in Charlestown pushes a box of mass 30 kg along a rough floor with a horizontal force of 120 N. The friction force is 45 N. Find the work done by (a) the vendor, (b) friction, and (c) the net work done, over a distance of 6 m.

Work by vendor is positive (force in direction of motion). Work by friction is negative (opposes motion). Net work = sum of all work done.

(a) 720 J   (b) −270 J   (c) 450 J

(a) Vendor: W = 120 × 6 = 720 J

(b) Friction: W = −45 × 6 = −270 J (negative — opposes motion)

(c) Net: 720 + (−270) = 450 J

Check: ΔKE should = 450 J if starting from rest, v = √(2×450/30) = √30 ≈ 5.48 m/s

Question 14 HARD
A cane cutter's truck of mass 2000 kg travels up a slope inclined at 10° to the horizontal. The engine produces a constant power of 40 kW and the resistance to motion (excluding gravity) is 800 N. Find the maximum speed up the slope. (g = 10 m/s²)

At max speed on a slope: driving force = friction resistance + weight component along slope. F = R + mg sinα. Then v_max = P/F.

v_max ≈ 11.1 m/s

Weight component along slope: mg sinα = 2000 × 10 × sin10° = 20000 × 0.1736 = 3472 N

Total resistance: F_total = 800 + 3472 = 4272 N

At max speed: P = F_total × v_max → v_max = 40000/4272 ≈ 9.36 m/s

(If using sin10° ≈ 0.174: mg sinα = 3480 N, F = 4280 N, v_max = 40000/4280 ≈ 9.35 m/s)

Question 15 HARD
A box of mass 5 kg is pushed 4 m up a rough slope inclined at 25° by a force of 60 N parallel to the slope. The friction force is 15 N. Using the work-energy principle, find the speed of the box at the top if it started from rest. (g = 10 m/s²)

Net work done by ALL forces = ΔKE. Forces along slope: applied (up +ve), weight component (down −ve), friction (down −ve).

v ≈ 3.35 m/s

Question 19 HARD
A roller-coaster at a Caribbean theme park starts from rest at a height of 30 m. At point B (height 12 m) the measured speed is 16 m/s. The car has mass 500 kg. Find the energy lost to friction between the start and point B. (g = 10 m/s²)

Energy lost to friction = (KE + GPE) at start − (KE + GPE) at B. If no friction, all GPE lost → KE. The difference is heat.

Energy lost to friction = 26 000 J = 26 kJ

Question 20 HARD
A bus on the Cayon road of mass 8000 kg descends a slope of 5° at constant speed 15 m/s. The engine is cut and only brakes are applied. The braking force is 12 000 N. Using the work-energy principle, find (a) the braking distance, and (b) the average power dissipated by the brakes during stopping. (g = 10 m/s²)

(a) Net work = ΔKE. Forces on slope: braking (up, opposing motion), gravity component (down the slope). Find net force then use W_net = ΔKE. (b) P_avg = work by brakes / time; find time from v_avg.

(a) d ≈ 108 m    (b) P_brakes ≈ 120 kW

KE lost: ½×8000×15² = 900 000 J

Weight along slope (down): mg sin5° = 8000×10×0.0872 = 6976 N (helps slow the bus)

Net stopping force: 12000 − 6976 = 5024 N (wait: weight component ASSISTS braking on a descent)

Actually: descending → gravity acts down-slope (same direction as motion), which REDUCES braking effect.

Net retarding force: 12 000 (brakes, up slope) − 6976 (gravity, down slope) = 5024 N

Net work = ΔKE: −5024 × d = −900 000 → d = 900 000/5024 ≈ 179 m

Work by brakes: 12 000 × 179 ≈ 2 148 000 J; Time = d/v_avg = 179/7.5 ≈ 23.9 s

Power by brakes: 2 148 000/23.9 ≈ 89.9 kW ≈ 90 kW
